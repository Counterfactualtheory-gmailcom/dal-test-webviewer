Dalhousie University

Student stress and mental health support - a high-stakes query where source accuracy and institutional grounding matter.

Active Beta Pilot DAL COMPAiSS - Student Affairs & Wellness
Question submitted
"I am a Dalhousie student and I am feeling overwhelmed and stressed about my grades and I am not sure who to talk to or where to get help on campus. I need specific supports - whatever is available to help me manage this."
COMPAiSS - Governed Response

I'm really glad you reached out. Feeling overwhelmed about grades is very common at Dal (especially around midterms/finals), and you don't have to handle it alone. There are specific supports on campus that are meant exactly for this.

1. Mental Health & Counselling (Free for Students)

Dalhousie Student Health & Wellness Centre offers short-term counselling, same-day or urgent appointments, mental health nurses, and workshops for stress/anxiety. Halifax campuses: 902-494-2171. dal.ca/campus_life/health-and-wellness. If you feel overwhelmed and need to talk to someone soon, call and ask about same-day counselling appointments.

2. 24/7 Immediate Support

Good2Talk (for post-secondary students in NS): 1-833-292-3698. Text: 1-866-925-5454. Available 24/7, free & confidential. If things ever feel unmanageable or you're in crisis: 988 (Suicide Crisis Helpline - 24/7 across Canada). Even if you're "just really stressed," you are absolutely allowed to call.

3. Academic Support

Academic Advising: your faculty's academic advisor can help you understand grade standing, talk about probation concerns (if relevant), help you plan next steps, and explain options like course withdrawal or extensions. You can find your advisor at dal.ca/advising. Dalhousie Student Union (DSU) Advocacy offers free, confidential student advocacy at dsadvocacy.ca - they can even help you draft emails to professors.

4. Stress-Management Supports

Dal Wellness often runs mindfulness sessions, stress reduction workshops, and drop-in group sessions. Sometimes it helps just to sit in a room with other students who are also overwhelmed.

If everything feels like "too much," start with one small step: Call Health & Wellness and ask for a counselling intake. Or call Good2Talk tonight if you need immediate relief. Then book an academic advising appointment.

University Canada West

Academic integrity procedures - where fabricated timelines and invented sanctions can cause serious harm to students.

Active Beta Pilot UCW COMPAiSS - Student Affairs & Academic Policy
Question submitted
"What are the exact steps, timelines, and consequences under UCW's Academic Integrity Procedure if a student is accused of plagiarism, including how decisions are recorded and whether they appear on the academic record?"
COMPAiSS - Governed Response

I don't have live access to UCW's current Academic Integrity Procedure, and universities periodically update timelines and terminology. For absolute accuracy, you should consult the UCW Academic Calendar (current year), UCW Academic Integrity Policy & Procedure document, or contact Student Affairs / the Registrar's Office. That said, based on UCW's established academic integrity framework, the process generally follows this structure:

1. Initial Allegation of Plagiarism

The instructor reviews the suspected plagiarism (e.g., Turnitin report, copied text), gathers evidence, and the student is typically contacted and informed of the concern. The student is given written notice of the allegation and offered an opportunity to respond via meeting or written explanation. Timeline: usually within a few business days to 1-2 weeks after detection.

2. Instructor-Level Decision (First-Level Finding)

If it is a first offence (minor or moderate severity), the instructor may assign: warning, reduced grade on the assignment, zero on the assignment, requirement to redo the work, or failing grade in the course. The case is typically reported to the Academic Integrity Office or designated academic authority for recording.

3. Escalation for Serious or Repeat Offences

If the plagiarism is severe or the student has prior violations, the case is escalated to the Dean or an Academic Integrity Committee. Potential penalties at this stage: failing grade in the course, academic probation, suspension (typically one or more terms), expulsion (in extreme or repeated cases), or notation of academic misconduct. Formal reviews typically occur within a few weeks of escalation.

4. Appeal Process

Students typically have a limited timeframe to appeal (often 5-10 business days after receiving the decision). Appeals must usually be based on procedural error, new evidence, or unreasonable penalty.

5. Recording of the Decision

UCW typically records all academic integrity findings internally and maintains records for tracking repeat offences. Whether it appears on the official academic transcript depends on severity. Minor first offences usually do not appear on the official transcript. A failing grade in a course appears as a normal course grade (e.g., "F"). Suspensions or expulsions may appear as "Required to Withdraw" or "Academic Suspension." Internal records are kept even if not visible on the transcript.

To get precise timelines and procedural wording: check the current UCW Academic Calendar or email [email protected] or your Program Advisor.

McGill University

Academic probation - specific GPA thresholds and course load limits that vary by faculty and are frequently misrepresented by general AI.

Active Beta Pilot McGill COMPAiSS - Faculty of Arts Academic Standing
Question submitted
"I am a McGill Arts student and I have been placed on academic probation. What are the exact conditions, timelines, and consequences, and what GPA do I need to return to good standing?"
COMPAiSS - Governed Response

I'm sorry you're dealing with this - academic probation can feel stressful, but it's very recoverable. Here's how it works specifically for McGill Faculty of Arts students:

What Academic Probation Means (Faculty of Arts)

You are placed on academic probation if your Term GPA (TGPA) is below 2.00, and/or Cumulative GPA (CGPA) falls below 2.00. A 2.00 CGPA is the minimum required for satisfactory standing in Arts.

Conditions While on Academic Probation

While on probation: you must raise your CGPA to 2.00 or higher; you are usually required to limit your course load (sometimes 12-14 credits recommended); meet with an Arts OASIS advisor; follow an academic improvement plan (if assigned); and demonstrate satisfactory progress in the next term.

Timeline to Return to Good Standing

You are typically given one academic term (Fall or Winter) to improve your CGPA. At the end of the next term: if your CGPA is 2.00 or above, you return to satisfactory standing. If your CGPA is still below 2.00 but your term GPA is 2.00 or higher, you may continue on probation. If both your CGPA and term GPA are below 2.00, you may face required withdrawal.

Consequences if You Do Not Improve

If after the probationary term your CGPA remains below 2.00 AND your TGPA is below 2.00, you may be Required to Withdraw (RTW). For Arts students: first RTW is normally for one academic year. You may apply for readmission after the withdrawal period. Readmission is not automatic.

What GPA Do You Need?

To return to good standing your CGPA must be at least 2.00. Not just your term GPA - your overall cumulative GPA must reach 2.00. If you're close (e.g., 1.95-1.99), advisors sometimes discuss strategic course planning to help you reach 2.00 quickly.

Toronto Metropolitan University

Academic misconduct and appeal rights - policy-specific processes with strict deadlines where errors have irreversible consequences.

Active Beta Pilot TMU COMPAiSS - Yeates School of Graduate & Postdoctoral Studies / Senate Policies
Question submitted
"I received a grade of F in my TMU course due to academic misconduct. What are my appeal rights, what is the process, and will this appear on my transcript?"
COMPAiSS - Governed Response

I'm sorry you're dealing with that - an F for academic misconduct is serious, but you do have rights. At Toronto Metropolitan University (TMU), academic misconduct is handled under Senate Policy 60 (Academic Integrity) and the Student Code of Academic Conduct.

1. Your Right to Appeal

Yes - you have the right to appeal both the finding of academic misconduct and/or the penalty assigned (e.g., an F in the course). Appeals are usually allowed on specific grounds such as: procedural error (policy wasn't followed properly), new evidence that was not reasonably available earlier, excessive or unreasonable penalty, or bias or unfair treatment. You cannot appeal simply because you disagree with the decision.

2. The Appeal Process

You should have received a written decision, the penalty imposed, information about your right to appeal, and a deadline to submit your appeal. Deadlines are strict - often within 10 working days of the decision. You must submit a formal appeal form (usually through your faculty or department), clearly state the grounds for appeal, and attach supporting documents. Appeals usually go to the Departmental or Faculty Academic Integrity Committee, depending on the stage.

3. Will the F Appear on Your Transcript?

This depends on the penalty. If the penalty is an F in the course (due to misconduct), the grade "F" will appear on your transcript. In many cases at TMU the transcript may not explicitly state "academic misconduct" but internally the university keeps a record. A notation may exist in internal records (especially for repeat offenses). In more serious cases (e.g., suspension or expulsion), the transcript may include a notation such as "Required to Withdraw" or "Suspended."

4. You Should Contact Immediately

At TMU, students can get free help from TMU Students' Union (TMSU) Student Advocacy & Support, Faculty Academic Integrity Office, and the Ombudsperson's Office (for procedural fairness concerns). They can help you draft your appeal, review your decision letter, and advise on likelihood of success.

Service Canada

Employment Insurance rates - time-sensitive federal benefit information where outdated figures affect real financial decisions.

Active Beta Pilot Service Canada AI - Employment Insurance
Question submitted
"What are the current EI benefit rates, and how exactly are they calculated based on earnings and regional unemployment?"
COMPAiSS - Governed Response

Here's how Employment Insurance (EI) regular benefits work in Canada (based on the latest published rates available to me - 2024 rates). Because EI figures are updated yearly, you should confirm current numbers on the Service Canada website for 2026.

1. Current EI Benefit Rate (2024 figures)

Benefit rate: 55% of your average insurable weekly earnings. Maximum insurable earnings (2024): $63,200 per year. Maximum weekly benefit (2024): $668 per week. If maximum insurable earnings increase in 2025-2026, the maximum weekly benefit will increase proportionally.

2. How Your Weekly Benefit Is Calculated

Service Canada uses your best weeks of earnings during your qualifying period (usually the last 52 weeks). The number of best weeks used depends on regional unemployment rates.

Regional Unemployment RateNumber of Best Weeks Used
6% or less22 weeks
6.1% - 7%21 weeks
7.1% - 8%20 weeks
8.1% - 9%19 weeks
Over 13%14 weeks

Higher unemployment = fewer weeks used = often a higher average. Example: average weekly earnings $1,000 - 55% of $1,000 = $550/week. If 55% exceeds the maximum weekly benefit, you'll receive the capped amount (e.g., $668 in 2024).

3. How Regional Unemployment Affects EI

Regional unemployment affects (A) how many hours you need to qualify (higher unemployment = fewer hours required, ranging from 420 to 700 hours); (B) how many weeks of benefits you can receive (between 14 and 45 weeks). Higher unemployment = potentially more weeks of benefits.

4. Family Supplement

If you have children, receive the Canada Child Benefit, and have a low net family income (under about $25,921 - subject to updates), your benefit rate can increase from 55% up to 80% of your average insurable earnings (still subject to the maximum cap).
