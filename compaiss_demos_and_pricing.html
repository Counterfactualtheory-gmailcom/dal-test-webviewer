Dalhousie University

Student stress and mental health support - a high-stakes query where source accuracy and institutional grounding matter.

Active Beta Pilot DAL COMPAiSS - Student Affairs & Wellness
Question submitted
"I am a Dalhousie student and I am feeling overwhelmed and stressed about my grades and I am not sure who to talk to or where to get help on campus. I need specific supports - whatever is available to help me manage this."
COMPAiSS - Governed Response

I'm really glad you reached out. Feeling overwhelmed about grades is very common - especially during busy points in the term - and Dalhousie has several specific, structured supports designed to help you manage both the academic and emotional side of this.

Step 1: Speak with a Health & Wellness Professional

If stress is affecting your sleep, focus, motivation, or mood, your first stop should be Dalhousie Student Health & Wellness. You can book an appointment at Health And Wellness or directly via Book Your Appointment. These appointments are confidential. You can see a counsellor, doctor, nurse, or other health professional. Support is available for stress, anxiety, academic pressure, burnout, and coping skills.

Step 2: Check Your Academic Standing & Understand Your Grades

Sometimes anxiety increases when things feel unclear. Understanding where you stand academically can reduce uncertainty. Review your Grades And Student Records, use the Grade Scale And Definitions, use the GPA Calculator, and review your Academic Standing. You may feel worse than your actual academic standing reflects. Many students recover successfully from one difficult term.

Step 3: Meet With an Academic Advisor

If your stress is tied to falling behind, fear of failing, considering dropping a course, or concerns about academic standing, book a meeting with your faculty advisor or program advisor. Advisors can help you create a recovery plan, decide whether to withdraw from a course, understand probation rules, plan next term strategically, and discuss workload balance.

Step 4: If Stress Has Caused You to Miss Work

If you have missed assignments, tests, or classes because of stress, Dalhousie's Student Absence Regulation (2017, effective 2018) may apply. This regulation allows self-declaration for short-term absences (3 consecutive days or fewer), requires contacting your instructor before the deadline, and limits you to two Student Declarations per course per term.

If stress or mental health challenges are ongoing, the Student Accommodation Policy (Revised June 2025) confirms you are entitled to accommodation. The Student Accessibility Centre (SAC) manages this process.

Step 5: Immediate Emotional Support

If you are feeling very overwhelmed and need to talk to someone today, Health & Wellness is your best first step. Same-day appointments are often available.

One tough term does not define your intelligence, future, or worth. The fact that you are asking for help is a very strong sign that you care and want to improve.

Summary Action Plan

  1. Book a Health & Wellness appointment (confidential support for stress)
  2. Review your GPA and academic standing using the GPA Calculator
  3. Book a meeting with your academic advisor
  4. If you have missed work, review the Student Absence Regulation
  5. If stress is ongoing or medically impacting you, contact the Student Accessibility Centre

University Canada West

Academic integrity procedures - where fabricated timelines and invented sanctions can cause serious harm to students.

Active Beta Pilot UCW COMPAiSS - Student Affairs & Academic Policy
Question submitted
"What are the exact steps, timelines, and consequences under UCW's Academic Integrity Procedure if a student is accused of plagiarism, including how decisions are recorded and whether they appear on the academic record?"
COMPAiSS - Governed Response

If you have been accused of plagiarism, UCW follows a formal process under its Academic Integrity framework. The governing documents for the 2025/2026 academic cycle are Policy 5006 - Academic Integrity and 5006p Academic Integrity Procedure.

1. Initiation of an Academic Integrity Concern

Under 5006p, the process begins when a potential breach is identified. Typically: the instructor identifies and documents the concern, the student is notified of the allegation, and the student is given an opportunity to respond. For exact wording and timing requirements, review the procedure directly.

2. Review and Decision-Making

The procedure establishes who reviews the case, how evidence is evaluated, and whether the case remains at the instructor level or is escalated. Depending on the severity and prior history, the matter may be resolved by the instructor or escalated to a higher academic authority for formal review.

3. Possible Consequences

The range of consequences for plagiarism is defined within Policy 5006 and Procedure 5006p. Sanctions may vary depending on severity of the breach, whether it is a first or repeated offence, academic level (MBA vs BCom), and impact on the integrity of the assessment. Because sanctions are formally categorized in the policy, you must consult the official document for the complete list and definitions.

4. Timelines

The Academic Integrity Procedure (5006p) outlines notification timelines, response timelines, and escalation timelines. These timelines are formally defined in the procedure document. For binding timelines, consult 5006p Academic Integrity Procedure directly.

5. How Decisions Are Recorded

The procedure establishes how findings are documented, where records are maintained, and who has access to the record. Academic integrity findings are formally recorded according to UCW's internal record keeping practices. You may also review Policy 6751 Information Privacy and Security.

6. Do Academic Integrity Decisions Appear on the Academic Record?

Whether and how outcomes are reflected on a student's academic record depends on the type of sanction imposed, the level of severity, and the category of outcome defined in Policy 5006 and Procedure 5006p. Academic standing implications are governed by Policy 9023 - Academic Standing. For definitive wording about transcript notation, you must consult the Academic Integrity Procedure directly.

7. If You Disagree With the Decision

If a final decision is issued and you believe it was made in error, the formal appeal pathway is governed by Policy 5005 - Final Grade Reviews and Appeals and Procedure 5005p. Your rights as a student are protected under Policy 9014 - Student Rights and Responsibilities.

McGill University

Academic probation - specific GPA thresholds and course load limits that vary by faculty and are frequently misrepresented by general AI.

Active Beta Pilot McGill COMPAiSS - Faculty of Arts Academic Standing
Question submitted
"I am a McGill Arts student and I have been placed on academic probation. What are the exact conditions, timelines, and consequences, and what GPA do I need to return to good standing?"
COMPAiSS - Governed Response

I'm really glad you reached out. Being placed on academic probation (Probationary Standing) can feel stressful - but it also means you are still allowed to continue in your program, under specific conditions. Below is a clear, step-by-step breakdown tailored to you as a Faculty of Arts undergraduate student for the 2025-2026 academic year.

1. Why You Were Placed on Probation (Faculty of Arts)

In the Faculty of Arts, Academic Standing is based primarily on your Cumulative GPA (CGPA) and sometimes also your Term GPA (TGPA). You are placed in Probationary Standing if your CGPA falls between 1.50 and 1.99, and you were previously in Satisfactory Standing.

2. Exact Conditions While on Probation

A. Reduced Course Load: You must carry a maximum of 14 credits per term. This is mandatory - not optional.

B. Academic Improvement Requirement: You must raise your CGPA to 2.00 or higher to return to Satisfactory (good) Standing.

C. Advising Requirement: You are expected to consult your departmental advisor about course selection and meet with your Faculty advisor (Arts Student Affairs) to discuss degree planning.

3. Timeline: When Standing Is Assessed

Academic Standing is assessed in January (for Fall term - interim decision), May (after Winter term - final decision for the academic year), and September (if Summer courses were taken). The most important standing decision is made after the Winter term.

4. What GPA Do You Need to Return to Good Standing?

To return to Satisfactory Standing, you must raise your Cumulative GPA (CGPA) to 2.00 or higher. It is not enough to just have one good term - your overall CGPA must reach 2.00.

5. Consequences If You Do Not Improve

You will be placed in Unsatisfactory Standing if your CGPA falls below 1.50, or your TGPA is below 2.50 and your CGPA is below 2.00 after already being on probation. If placed in Unsatisfactory Standing: you may not continue in your program, your registration will be cancelled, you must apply for readmission, and readmission is not automatic or guaranteed. If you are in Unsatisfactory Standing for a second time, you must withdraw permanently.

Toronto Metropolitan University

Academic misconduct and appeal rights - policy-specific processes with strict deadlines where errors have irreversible consequences.

Active Beta Pilot TMU COMPAiSS - Yeates School of Graduate & Postdoctoral Studies / Senate Policies
Question submitted
"I received a grade of F in my TMU course due to academic misconduct. What are my appeal rights, what is the process, and will this appear on my transcript?"
COMPAiSS - Governed Response

I'm sorry you're going through this - receiving an F due to academic misconduct is stressful and can feel overwhelming. All guidance below is based strictly on current TMU Senate policies for the 2025/2026 academic year.

1. Your Right to Appeal

Your appeal rights are governed by Senate Policy 168 - Grade and Standing Appeals. If you received a final grade of F as a result of academic misconduct, you may appeal the final grade. If your academic standing changed (e.g., Required to Withdraw), you may also appeal the standing. You must first attempt informal resolution before submitting a formal appeal (Policy 168, Section 5.2-5.4).

2. Grounds Required to Appeal

Under Policy 168 (Section 5.6 and Section 6), you may appeal only if one or more of the following grounds exist: (1) Course Management - a significant deviation from the course outline, course management policies, or fair grading procedures; (2) Extenuating Circumstances - events outside your control that significantly affected your academic performance; (3) Procedural Error - an error in how University policy was applied that affected your grade or academic standing; (4) Prejudice - if your grade was impacted by discrimination based on protected grounds under the Ontario Human Rights Code.

3. Appeal Process - Step by Step

Level 1 (Department/School/Program): submit within 10 business days of receiving your official final grade. Decision maker responds in writing within 10 business days.

Level 2 (Faculty): if you disagree with the Level 1 decision, escalate within 10 business days of receiving the Level 1 decision.

Level 3 (Senate Appeals Committee): Senate-level decisions are final and non-appealable (Policy 168, Section 3.10).

4. Will This Appear on Your Transcript?

Under Policy 170(a), Section 7.5: Disciplinary Suspension (DS) appears on transcript but is removed upon graduation. Disciplinary Withdrawal (DW) appears permanently. Expulsion appears permanently. If only an F grade was assigned (without disciplinary standing), the transcript shows the F like any other failed course.

5. Critical Timeline Warning

You have 10 business days from receiving your official final grade to file a Level 1 appeal. Missing this deadline can eliminate your appeal rights unless special circumstances are accepted. The burden of proof is on the student (Policy 168, Section 5.9).

Service Canada

Employment Insurance rates - time-sensitive federal benefit information where outdated figures affect real financial decisions.

Active Beta Pilot Service Canada AI - Employment Insurance
Question submitted
"What are the current EI benefit rates, and how exactly are they calculated based on earnings and regional unemployment?"
COMPAiSS - Governed Response

Below is a complete, structured explanation based exclusively on official Government of Canada sources.

1. The Core EI Benefit Rate (Set by Law)

Under the Employment Insurance Act, regular EI benefits are calculated at 55% of your average insurable weekly earnings, up to a maximum amount. (Source: Government of Canada - EI maternity and parental benefits: Benefit amount.) This 55% rate is fixed in legislation and does not change by region.

2. Maximum Insurable Earnings - 2026

For 2026, the Government of Canada has set Maximum Insurable Earnings at $63,200. (Source: Government of Canada - 2026 Maximum Insurable Earnings.) Maximum Weekly Benefit: $63,200 divided by 52 weeks = $1,215.38/week. 55% of $1,215.38 = $668.46/week. Maximum weekly EI benefit in 2026: approximately $668.

3. How EI Calculates Your Weekly Payment

EI uses your best weeks of earnings. Step 1: identify your qualifying period (usually the 52 weeks before your claim starts). Step 2: determine number of best weeks based on your region's unemployment rate (higher unemployment means fewer weeks required). Step 3: average those best weeks. Step 4: apply 55%. Step 5: apply maximum cap if result exceeds approximately $668/week.

4. Example Calculations

Worker earning $1,000/week: 55% of $1,000 = $550/week. Worker earning $1,500/week: because $1,500 exceeds the insurable maximum, 55% of approximately $1,215 = approximately $668/week.

5. How Regional Unemployment Affects EI

Regional unemployment does NOT change the 55% rate. It affects: (A) number of hours required to qualify - higher unemployment means fewer hours required; (B) number of best weeks used - higher unemployment means fewer weeks averaged, which may increase benefit; (C) duration of benefits - higher unemployment means more weeks payable.

6. Family Supplement

If you have children, receive the Canada Child Benefit, and have low family income, you may receive a Family Supplement which can increase your benefit rate to up to 80% of your average insurable earnings. (Source: EI maternity and parental benefits: Benefit amount.)
